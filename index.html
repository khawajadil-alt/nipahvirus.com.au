01

What Is Nipah Virus?

Virology, classification & global significance

Classification

RNA Virus

Nipah virus (NiV) belongs to the Henipavirus genus within the family Paramyxoviridae. It is a single-stranded, negative-sense RNA virus — the same broad family as measles and mumps, but far more lethal to humans.

Discovery

First identified in 1999 during an outbreak in Kampung Sungai Nipah, Malaysia — from which the virus takes its name. The outbreak affected pig farmers and caused 265 human cases and 105 deaths. Over a million pigs were culled to contain the spread.

Why It Matters

The World Health Organization (WHO) lists Nipah virus on its Blueprint priority pathogens — diseases with epidemic or pandemic potential that urgently require accelerated research. Its high case fatality rate and human-to-human transmission capability make it a serious global health threat.

Geographic Range

Emerging

Outbreaks have occurred primarily in South and Southeast Asia — Malaysia, Singapore, Bangladesh, and India. The natural host range of Pteropus fruit bats spans a much wider region, suggesting significant potential for geographic spread.

02

How It Spreads

Zoonotic spillover & human-to-human dynamics

Nipah virus is a zoonotic pathogen — it naturally resides in animals and spills over into humans through various exposure routes. Unlike many viruses, it can also spread directly between people.

  • 01
    Bat-to-Human (Direct)
    Direct contact with infected fruit bats (Pteropus spp.) or their bodily fluids, urine, feces, or partially-eaten fruit. A primary route in Bangladesh and India.
  • 02
    Contaminated Food or Drink
    Consuming raw date palm sap or fresh fruit contaminated with bat secretions. This has been the most common route in Bangladeshi outbreaks, particularly with raw date palm juice collected overnight.
  • 03
    Animal-to-Human (Intermediate Hosts)
    In Malaysia's 1999 outbreak, pigs served as amplifying hosts. Farmers contracted the virus through direct contact with infected pigs' secretions and tissues.
  • 04
    Human-to-Human Transmission
    Documented in Bangladesh and India outbreaks. Spread occurs through close contact with infected persons' respiratory secretions, bodily fluids, or contaminated surfaces. Healthcare workers are at particular risk.
Natural Reservoir

Pteropus Fruit Bats

🦇

Large fruit bats of the genus Pteropus (also called flying foxes) are the natural animal reservoir for Nipah virus. Crucially, these bats do not become ill from the virus — they carry it asymptomatically.

Their range across South and Southeast Asia, South Asia, and parts of Africa and Australia defines the broad zone of potential NiV spillover risk. Habitat destruction bringing bats into closer contact with livestock and humans increases risk.

Clinical Presentation

Symptom progression from initial infection to severe encephalitis

Incubation — Days 4 to 14
No Symptoms
The incubation period ranges from 4 to 14 days, though cases of up to 45 days have been reported. During this period, the infected person shows no signs of illness but the virus is replicating.
Early Phase — Days 1 to 3 of Illness
Influenza-like Onset
Initial symptoms are nonspecific and may resemble influenza, making early diagnosis challenging.
Fever Headache Myalgia Vomiting Sore Throat Fatigue
Middle Phase — Days 3 to 7
Neurological Involvement
In severe cases, the virus crosses the blood-brain barrier causing encephalitis — inflammation of the brain. This phase is where NiV distinguishes itself from common respiratory illnesses.
Dizziness Drowsiness Altered Consciousness Disorientation Atypical Pneumonia
Severe Phase — Days 7 to 10+
Encephalitis & Coma
Without treatment, severe cases progress rapidly to acute encephalitis, seizures, and coma. Death can occur within 24–48 hours of onset of severe neurological symptoms.
Seizures Coma Cerebral Edema Respiratory Failure Death (40–75%)
Long-Term — Survivors
Relapsing Encephalitis
Some survivors experience long-term neurological sequelae including personality changes, persistent seizure disorders, and — notably — relapsing encephalitis months to years after apparent recovery. The virus can remain latent in the nervous system.

Prevention &
Control

Public health measures, treatment & outbreak response

01
Avoid Exposure to Bats
Do not consume raw date palm sap, fresh fruit that may have been partially eaten by bats, or handle bat carcasses without protective equipment. Cover date palm juice collecting pots overnight.
02
Infection Control in Hospitals
Healthcare workers must use standard contact and droplet precautions including gowns, gloves, masks, and eye protection when treating suspected NiV patients. Isolate confirmed cases immediately.
03
Food Safety Practices
Wash all fruit thoroughly before consumption. Avoid fruit with bite marks or damage. Boiling raw date palm sap kills the virus. Cooking meat thoroughly reduces transmission risk from animal products.
04
Surveillance & Contact Tracing
Robust surveillance systems in high-risk regions like Bangladesh and Kerala have been essential for rapid outbreak detection. Aggressive contact tracing — as seen in Kerala's 2018 response — can break transmission chains.
05
Supportive Medical Care
There is currently no approved antiviral treatment. Intensive supportive care including mechanical ventilation, seizure management, and care for cerebral edema remains the standard approach for severe cases.
06
Community Education
Public awareness campaigns in endemic areas have significantly reduced date palm sap transmission in Bangladesh. Educating communities about bat contact risks and early symptom recognition saves lives.
⚠
No Licensed Vaccine or Treatment
As of now, there is no WHO-approved vaccine or antiviral treatment for Nipah virus. Ribavirin has shown some benefit in limited studies but is not conclusively proven. The monoclonal antibody m102.4 is in clinical trials. Prevention remains the primary defense.

Diagnosis &
Treatment

Laboratory confirmation methods, clinical management & investigational therapies

Diagnosis

Real-Time PCR (RT-PCR)

The gold-standard diagnostic during the acute phase. RT-PCR can detect NiV RNA in throat swabs, nasal swabs, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), urine, and blood. Results can be available within hours and are critical for early containment decisions.

Diagnosis

Serology — ELISA

Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) testing for NiV-specific IgM and IgG antibodies. Used for confirmation and retrospective diagnosis — particularly useful when acute-phase samples are unavailable. IgM is typically detectable within days of symptom onset.

Treatment

Supportive Intensive Care

There is no specific approved antiviral treatment for Nipah virus. Management centres on intensive supportive care: management of encephalitis, control of seizures, mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure, and fluid management. Early isolation and infection control are essential.

Investigational

Monoclonal Antibody m102.4

The humanised monoclonal antibody m102.4 was administered on compassionate-use grounds during the 2018 and 2023 Kerala outbreaks. It targets the NiV G glycoprotein and has shown promise in animal models. Phase I human safety trials have been completed. Not yet approved for routine clinical use.

Investigational

Ribavirin

The broad-spectrum antiviral ribavirin has shown in-vitro activity against NiV and was used during the 1999 Malaysia outbreak with some evidence of reduced mortality. However, clinical evidence remains limited and it is not a standard-of-care treatment. WHO continues to recommend its evaluation in future outbreaks.

Diagnosis

Virus Isolation & Neutralisation

Virus isolation and plaque reduction neutralisation tests (PRNT) can be performed at BSL-4 reference laboratories. Immunohistochemistry on post-mortem tissue is also used for confirmation. Due to the high biosafety requirements, these tests are restricted to specialist facilities such as CSIRO's ACDP in Geelong, Australia.

⚠️
Clinical Note
Early clinical presentation of Nipah virus is non-specific and easily confused with influenza or meningitis. In endemic regions, clinicians should consider NiV in any patient with fever and altered mental status, particularly with a history of bat exposure, raw date palm sap consumption, or contact with a confirmed case. Early PCR testing and isolation are critical.
06

Research &
Pandemic Potential

Current scientific progress and global preparedness

Vaccine Development

mRNA & Subunit Approaches

Several vaccine candidates are in preclinical and early clinical trial phases. The Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations (CEPI) is funding multiple NiV vaccine programs. mRNA-based vaccines — similar to COVID-19 vaccines — are among the leading candidates being studied by Moderna and other manufacturers.

Antiviral Therapy

Monoclonal Antibodies

The human monoclonal antibody m102.4, targeting the NiV G glycoprotein used for cell entry, has shown protection in primate models and was compassionately used in the 2023 Kerala outbreak. Phase 1 safety trials have been completed. Remdesivir, used for other RNA viruses, is also under evaluation.

Pandemic Risk

Why Scientists Are Alarmed

NiV combines several dangerous features: a very high case fatality rate, demonstrated human-to-human transmission, a wide natural reservoir host range, no approved countermeasures, and a large potential exposure population in densely populated South Asia. Some virologists consider it among the most credible pandemic threats.

Ecology & Surveillance

One Health Approach

Understanding NiV requires integrating human, animal, and environmental health — the "One Health" framework. Research programs are tracking NiV seroprevalence in bat populations across Asia and Africa, monitoring for novel spillover events, and studying how climate change and deforestation affect bat-human interfaces.

Nipah & Australia

Australian risk, research, and the Hendra connection

Nipah virus has never been detected in Australia — but the country is far from unaffected by the henipavirus family. Australia's flying foxes carry the closely related Hendra virus, and CSIRO researchers are at the forefront of global NiV preparedness.

Australian fruit bats — specifically Pteropus flying foxes — are the same genus that acts as the natural reservoir for Nipah virus across Asia. While NiV itself has not been found in Australian bat populations, scientific risk assessments have found that NiV is present in flying foxes in some of Australia's nearest neighbours, including parts of Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, and Timor-Leste.

A formal risk assessment conducted by Australian wildlife health authorities found the probability of NiV establishing in Australian flying foxes through cross-border bat movements to be very low to low — though experts note a high level of uncertainty given the limited data. Habitat destruction, climate change, and increased human-wildlife contact are factors that could alter this risk over time.

The Burnet Institute has confirmed that for Australians, the immediate risk from current overseas outbreaks remains very low. Australia maintains strong disease surveillance and response systems, and Health Minister Mark Butler confirmed in January 2026 that authorities were monitoring the West Bengal outbreak "very, very closely."

In a notable historical connection, the experimental monoclonal antibody m102.4 — developed and held in Australia — was exported to Kerala, India during the deadly 2018 outbreak, where it was used compassionately in treatment attempts. Australia has played a direct role in global NiV response.

Current Australian Risk

Assessment — March 2026

Nipah virus has never been reported within Australia and there are currently no cases. Australia's biosurveillance infrastructure is robust and prepared for emerging zoonotic threats.

The closely related Hendra virus is present in Australia, carried by flying foxes. Hendra requires bat → horse → human transmission; there is no evidence of direct bat-to-human spread.

CSIRO advises the public: do not handle bats. Contact a wildlife rescue organisation if you encounter an injured or distressed flying fox.

Risk to Australian public: Very Low
🦇
Flying Fox Connection
Australian Pteropus flying foxes are the same bat genus that hosts NiV in Asia. Researchers at CSIRO have identified 24 new henipavirus species in Australian flying foxes sampled between 2018–2021, including the newly discovered "Salt Gully virus" — a novel henipavirus found in Queensland bat urine, circulating undetected since at least 2011.
🔬
CSIRO Research Leadership
Australia's CSIRO operates the Australian Centre for Disease Preparedness (ACDP) — one of only a handful of Biosafety Level 4 laboratories in the world capable of safely studying NiV and Hendra. CSIRO is advancing diagnostics, surveillance tools, and early-stage NiV vaccines. Their researchers wear fully encapsulating suits when handling these pathogens.
🐴
Hendra Virus in Australia
Hendra virus — NiV's closest known relative — is an established, notifiable disease in Australia. It spills over from flying foxes into horses, and from horses to humans. A licensed equine vaccine exists. Habitat loss driven by climate change and deforestation is known to influence the rate of bat-to-horse spillover events, particularly during nutrient-poor dry seasons.
🌏
Travel Advisory
Australians travelling to South or Southeast Asia — particularly Bangladesh, India (especially Kerala and West Bengal), and neighbouring regions — should avoid contact with bats, pigs, and raw date palm sap. NiV outbreaks occur almost annually in parts of these regions. Seek medical attention promptly if fever or neurological symptoms develop within 45 days of return.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about Nipah virus answered

What is Nipah virus?

Nipah virus (NiV) is a zoonotic RNA virus in the Henipavirus genus, family Paramyxoviridae. It causes severe encephalitis and respiratory illness in humans, with a case fatality rate of 40–75%. The natural reservoir is Pteropus fruit bats. It is classified by the WHO as a priority pathogen due to its pandemic potential and lack of approved vaccines or treatments.

What are the symptoms of Nipah virus?

Early symptoms include fever, headache, muscle pain (myalgia), vomiting and sore throat — similar to influenza. In severe cases the virus causes encephalitis (brain inflammation), leading to dizziness, drowsiness, altered consciousness, seizures, coma, and death. The case fatality rate is 40–75%. Some survivors experience relapsing encephalitis months or years later.

Is Nipah virus airborne?

Nipah virus is not considered efficiently airborne. It spreads primarily through direct contact with infected bodily secretions (saliva, urine, blood, respiratory secretions). Droplet transmission in close-contact healthcare settings has been documented. Standard droplet and contact precautions are recommended for healthcare workers — full airborne (N95) precautions are applied when performing aerosol-generating procedures.

Is there a treatment or cure for Nipah virus?

There is no specific approved treatment for Nipah virus as of 2026. Management is supportive — intensive care for encephalitis, seizure control, mechanical ventilation when needed. The experimental monoclonal antibody m102.4 has been used on compassionate grounds and is in human trials. Ribavirin showed some benefit in early studies but has not been validated in controlled trials.

How is Nipah virus diagnosed?

During the acute phase, RT-PCR on throat swabs, nasal swabs, cerebrospinal fluid, blood or urine is the primary diagnostic method. ELISA serology (NiV-specific IgM and IgG antibodies) is used for confirmation or retrospective diagnosis. Virus isolation requires BSL-4 facilities. Early diagnosis is challenging because initial symptoms overlap with many other febrile illnesses.

Is there a Nipah virus vaccine?

No vaccine is approved for Nipah virus as of 2026. The most advanced candidate is the Oxford University ChAdOx1 NipahB vaccine, now in Phase I human trials with CEPI funding. Merck's mRNA platform is also in preclinical development. Given NiV's sporadic outbreak pattern, vaccine trials are logistically challenging — CEPI and WHO are coordinating adaptive trial protocols.
